# Daniel Solove — privacy is plural, not singular

> Privacy is not one thing with a single essence but a plurality of distinct, related
> problems; to protect it you must name the specific activity that does the harm —
> not invoke "privacy" as a whole and hope the word does the work.

## Anchor

- **Axiom** — There is no single definition of privacy; it is a family of related problems, and the only useful question is *which* specific privacy harm a given activity commits.
- **Idioms**
  - *A Taxonomy of Privacy* — a bottom-up map of privacy as four families of harmful activity: information collection, information processing, information dissemination, and invasion.
  - *Aggregation* — the harm of combining individually innocuous pieces of data into a revealing whole.
  - *Secondary use* — using data gathered for one purpose for an unrelated second purpose without consent.
  - *Exclusion* — the harm of keeping people ignorant of, and shut out from, how their own data is held and used.
  - *Family resemblances* — privacy has no common denominator; its problems are related the way games are (Wittgenstein), by overlapping similarity, not a shared essence.
  - *The "nothing to hide" argument* — the claim that only wrongdoers fear surveillance; Solove's rebuttal: it assumes privacy is about hiding bad acts, when most privacy harms have nothing to do with concealment.
- **Tenets**
  1. The century-long hunt for the essence of privacy failed; that is why privacy is "a concept in disarray."
  2. Privacy should be conceptualized bottom-up from concrete problems, not top-down from a definition.
  3. The right question is never "is this private?" but "which specific privacy problem does this activity create?"
  4. Harms like aggregation and exclusion prove privacy can be violated without any secret being revealed.
  5. "Nothing to hide" is a category error: it answers a question about concealment that most privacy problems never asked.

## Orientation

Daniel J. Solove is a law professor at George Washington University and the most-cited privacy-law scholar of his generation. His 2006 article *A Taxonomy of Privacy* and his 2008 book *Understanding Privacy* gave the field its working vocabulary — a map of sixteen distinct privacy problems grouped into four kinds of activity — and his dismantling of the "nothing to hide" argument is the standard reply to privacy's most common dismissal. Where others sought the one true meaning of privacy, Solove built the catalogue of what actually goes wrong.

## Doctrine — the theory

Solove is a pragmatist about concepts, in the lineage of Dewey and Wittgenstein. He refuses the philosopher's search for the *essence* of privacy because that search has a perfect record of failure: every candidate — the right to be let alone, secrecy, control over information, intimacy — either sweeps in too much or leaves out too much. So he inverts the method. Instead of defining privacy and then deducing which problems count, he starts from the concrete activities people actually experience as violations and groups them by family resemblance. Privacy is not a thing; it is a set of related problems that share no single common feature.

The result is a taxonomy organized along the life of data: **information collection** (surveillance, interrogation), **information processing** (aggregation, identification, insecurity, secondary use, exclusion), **information dissemination** (breach of confidentiality, disclosure, exposure, increased accessibility, blackmail, appropriation, distortion), and **invasion** (intrusion, decisional interference). Sixteen named harms, each with its own logic of why it is bad and what remedy fits. The payoff is diagnostic precision: you stop arguing whether something "violates privacy" in the abstract and instead locate the exact harm — *is this aggregation, or secondary use, or exclusion, or a breach of confidentiality?* — and the answer tells you what to do about it.

The taxonomy also disarms bad arguments. "Nothing to hide" only ever rebuts the *disclosure* and *exposure* harms — the ones about concealing embarrassing facts. It is simply irrelevant to aggregation, identification, secondary use, or exclusion, none of which require you to have anything to hide. That is why the argument feels intuitively strong and is nonetheless wrong: it quietly shrinks all of privacy down to a single one of its sixteen problems. Hand Solove a system he never saw and the move is always the same — refuse the global verdict, name the local harm.

**Why the lens:** because "does this violate privacy?" is unanswerable and useless — the operative question is *which* of privacy's distinct harms an activity commits, and that one you can actually answer and fix.

## The lens

Point this lens at a data-handling design and ask:
- For each activity in this system, name the *specific* harm from the taxonomy — surveillance, aggregation, secondary use, exclusion, disclosure — instead of asking whether it "violates privacy."
- Where does this combine innocuous data into a revealing aggregate the subject never consented to assemble?
- Is data collected for one stated purpose being put to a second, unrelated use?
- Are the people in this data shut out from knowing how it is held and used (exclusion) — and would a "nothing to hide" framing make you miss that real harm entirely?

## Canon

- "A Taxonomy of Privacy" (*University of Pennsylvania Law Review*, vol. 154, 2006) — the article; the four groups and sixteen harms.
- *Understanding Privacy* (Harvard University Press, 2008) — the book; the pragmatic, family-resemblance method behind the taxonomy.
- *Nothing to Hide: The False Tradeoff Between Privacy and Security* (2011) — the full case against the "nothing to hide" argument.
