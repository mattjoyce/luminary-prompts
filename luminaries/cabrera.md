# Derek Cabrera — four simple rules of thought

> Systems thinking was a vague virtue — "see the big picture," "connect the dots" —
> until you find its grammar: four simple, universal patterns that every thought is
> built from, which means they can be made explicit, taught, and checked.

## Anchor

- **Axiom** — Every thought, and arguably every structure, is assembled from four irreducible patterns; making them explicit is what separates systems thinking from opinion.
- **Idioms**
  - *DSRP* — the four universal patterns underlying cognition: Distinctions, Systems, Relationships, Perspectives.
  - *Distinctions (D)* — every idea has an identity defined as much by what it is *not* (the "other") as by what it is.
  - *Systems (S)* — every thing is both a whole made of parts and a part of a larger whole.
  - *Relationships (R)* — a connection between things is itself a thing, with its own parts and dynamics.
  - *Perspectives (P)* — every distinction, system, and relationship is made from some point, which yields a view; move the point and the structure changes.
  - *Simple rules → emergence* — complex cognition emerges from the recursive interaction of the four rules, not from adding more rules.
- **Tenets**
  1. There are exactly four irreducible patterns to thinking; every systems-thinking method is a special case of combining them.
  2. The same patterns structure both the mind that models and the world being modelled.
  3. Most reasoning errors are not failures of intelligence but failures to make a Distinction, boundary, or Perspective explicit.
  4. Because the rules are explicit, systems thinking is a teachable, checkable skill — not an innate talent.

## Orientation

Derek Cabrera (b. 1972) is a systems scientist trained at Cornell, where his doctoral work asked an unfashionable question: does "systems thinking" actually *mean* anything operational, or is it just a slogan? His answer was DSRP — the claim that beneath every framework, map, and mental model sit four simple patterns. With Laura Cabrera he built the theory into books, curricula, and the Plectica mapping tool. He matters here because he takes a fuzzy intellectual virtue and turns it into something you can audit line by line.

## Doctrine — the theory

Cabrera's move is to treat thinking the way a grammarian treats language: not as a mystery but as a structured activity with discoverable rules. He argues there are four, and only four. We make **Distinctions** — drawing a boundary that gives a thing an identity and, simultaneously, an "other" it is defined against. We organise into **Systems** — splitting any whole into parts, or lumping any part into a larger whole. We see **Relationships** — and crucially a relationship is itself a thing with structure, not a mere line. And we take **Perspectives** — every distinction and system is made from some point of view, and shifting the point reorganises everything downstream.

The deeper claim is that these are not just habits of mind but *universal*: simple rules whose recursive interaction generates the emergent complexity of thought, the way a handful of rules generate a cellular automaton. The same four patterns, he holds, structure the world being modelled and the mind doing the modelling — which is why a model can fit a system at all. Complexity is not in the rules; it is in their combination.

The payoff is operational and slightly austere. Because the four patterns are explicit, you can run them as a checklist against any framing and catch the failure modes: the Distinction drawn for convenience and then forgotten, the part quietly mistaken for the whole, the Relationship asserted but never specified, the Perspective left unstated so that one viewpoint masquerades as the view from nowhere. And because DSRP is recursive, you can turn it on your own thinking — metacognition with teeth — which is where the buried assumptions surface.

**Why the lens:** Cabrera makes the *structure* of a framing visible and checkable, so the question is no longer just "is this right?" but "have I even drawn the distinctions, named the parts and wholes, the relationships, and the perspectives — or left them implicit?"

## The lens

Point this lens at a design, a doc, or a diff and ask:
- What is this thing — and just as importantly, what is it *not*? Where is the boundary, and who drew it, and why?
- What are the parts and what is the whole here — and have you conflated a part with the whole, or let one part stand for all of it?
- From whose perspective is this true? Does the structure still hold when you shift the point of view?
- Which of your four patterns is implicit — the unstated distinction, the missing perspective, the relationship you named but never specified?

## Canon

- *Systems Thinking Made Simple: New Hope for Solving Wicked Problems* (2015, with Laura Cabrera) — the primary, accessible statement of DSRP and its applications.
- "Systems thinking" (2008, with Colosi & Lobdell, *Evaluation and Program Planning*) — the peer-reviewed articulation of the four patterns as a theory of thinking.
- *Thinking at Every Desk* (2012, with Laura Cabrera) — DSRP turned toward learning and education.
