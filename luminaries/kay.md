# Alan Kay — a new medium for human thought

> The computer is not a faster typewriter or a better filing cabinet — it is a new
> medium, as the printing press was, and we have barely begun to learn to think in
> it; build something a person can think *with*, not the paperwork version of what
> they did before.

## Anchor

- **Axiom** — The computer is a metamedium that can become any medium, including ones not yet invented; squander that on digitised paper and you have wasted the only thing that makes it new.
- **Idioms**
  - *Object-oriented programming* — his term; he meant only messaging, local retention and protection and hiding of state-process, and extreme late-binding — not classes and inheritance.
  - *Dynabook* — a 1972 vision of a book-sized personal computer as a dynamic medium a child thinks with, not a productivity appliance.
  - *Metamedium / personal dynamic media* — the computer as a medium able to simulate and become every other medium, present and future.
- **Tenets**
  1. The computer's essence is that it can simulate any medium and ones unimagined; building "digital paper" throws that away.
  2. A change in perspective is worth ~80 IQ points — the right representation collapses a hard problem, so design the point of view, not just the feature.
  3. The best way to predict the future is to invent it; the interesting future is built, not forecast.
  4. The computer revolution hasn't happened yet — we mostly automated the past instead of amplifying new kinds of thought.
  5. OOP is messaging and late binding between independent objects, not classes; most of what's called "object-oriented" misses the point.

## Orientation

Alan Kay (b. 1940) led the Learning Research Group at Xerox PARC that built Smalltalk and the overlapping-window graphical interface, coined "object-oriented programming," and envisioned the Dynabook — the conceptual ancestor of every laptop and tablet. He won the 2003 Turing Award for that body of work. His enduring argument is not about any one machine but about what computing is *for*: a new medium for thinking, on the scale of writing or print, that we are still learning to use.

## Doctrine — the theory

Kay's worldview begins with media, not machines. Reading McLuhan, he took seriously that a medium reshapes the thought of anyone who internalises it — and that the computer is the first *metamedium*, one that can become all the others and invent new ones besides. So the question he asks of any system is never "is it fast or convenient?" but "what new way of thinking does this make possible that was impossible before?" A spreadsheet that only mimics a paper ledger has failed that test even if it works perfectly.

The second move is about representation. Borrowing from Bruner and Piaget — and from his own work designing for children — Kay holds that difficulty lives largely in the point of view. Find the representation that fits the problem and the problem nearly dissolves; this is what "a change in perspective is worth 80 IQ points" means operationally. Design is therefore the search for the right perspective to hand the user, not merely the arrangement of controls.

The third move is temporal and defiant. Because the medium is genuinely new, you cannot extrapolate the future from the present — you have to build it. "The best way to predict the future is to invent it" is not a motivational poster to Kay; it is the only honest stance toward a medium whose possibilities haven't been discovered yet. Hence his standing complaint that the computer revolution hasn't happened: we keep paving the cow-paths of old media instead of exploring what the metamedium can newly do.

**Why the lens:** Kay forces the prior question — *is this a new medium the user can think in, or just the old medium reimplemented in pixels?*

## Concepts

| Concept | What it means | Source |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| Object-oriented programming | Computation as independent objects communicating by messages, with hidden local state and late binding | coined ~1967; Smalltalk at Xerox PARC, 1970s |
| Dynabook | A book-sized personal computer as a dynamic medium for learning and thought | "A Personal Computer for Children of All Ages" (1972) |
| Metamedium | The computer as a medium that can simulate and become all other media | "Personal Dynamic Media" (Kay & Goldberg, 1977) |
| Late binding | Deferring decisions — types, meanings, connections — to the last moment to keep systems malleable | "The Early History of Smalltalk" (1993) |
| Point of view ≈ 80 IQ points | The right representation or perspective collapses a problem's difficulty | Kay, talks |

## The lens

Point this lens at a design or implementation and ask:
- Is this a new medium the user can think *in*, or just the paper/desktop version reimplemented in pixels?
- What point of view would make this problem simple — and are we designing the representation, or only the feature?
- What can the user now do that was impossible before the computer, or have we just made an old task faster?
- If a curious child sat down with this, would it invite them to build and explore, or only to consume?

## Canon

- "A Personal Computer for Children of All Ages" (1972) — the Dynabook paper; the vision in full.
- "Personal Dynamic Media" (Kay & Goldberg, 1977) — the metamedium argument; the computer as the medium that becomes all media.
- "The Early History of Smalltalk" (1993) — what OOP actually meant, in his own words.
- "The Computer Revolution Hasn't Happened Yet" (OOPSLA keynote, 1997) — the standing indictment of automating the past.
